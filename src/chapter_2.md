# what makes elegant ttrpg design?

The best definition I've ever seen for ttrpgs was describing them as "formally structured imagination" ([GrismundGames](https://www.reddit.com/r/RPGdesign/comments/ue3t42/comment/i6ljryr/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=web3x&utm_name=web3xcss&utm_term=1&utm_content=share_button)) --- it perfectly encapsulates two things: the experience we want the GM to deliver and the constraints needed for decision-making.
In essence, what sets ttrpgs apart is the interactive freedom of creativity. On the one hand, we want to utilize these flexible bounds, but at the same time, we have to implement some restrictions that force the players to balance their decisions meaningfully. Such decisions involve risk and impact on the experience.
So generally, we want unobtrusive mechanics, namely ones that deliver the intended experience with as few in-between steps as possible but that live up to the wanted experience through the decisions made.
Before examining how to create mechanics supporting this kind of approach I would like to anticipate that this concept does not put one type of game over another. For example, tactical games strive for a different experience than story games and have different problems to solve, so their rules will naturally stray. The important takeaway is that elegance is not a statement about the complexity of a system but about the amount of useless interactions with respect to the core experience.

And now onto how to implement this philosophy.
The key aspects are the variables of your game and their presentation. First of all, we want few variables that have to be referred to when using a mechanic. But just removing some from the game without compensation would without question make it more shallow in most cases. Giving one variable multiple functions quickly becomes fiddly yet even reduces the amount of interesting interactions. Instead, we will build our system in a spiraling form and assign every element to either the active function of a new variable or reduce it to a passive interaction with one.
Starting from the core of your system (probably the resolution mechanic) you build the next most important elements one after the other. Always ask yourself whether an entirely new variable is needed or instead an existing one can be manipulated to enhance the experience. When deciding on manipulating another variable think of a way so that the information needed can be seen at a glance and no steps that aren't part of the experience have to be taken.

Some examples of how this looks like:
- In Crown and Skull instead of taking damage, you lose equipment or skills, by crossing them off, making this hindrance immediately visible without having to refer to another variable like wounds or a health value.
- In Blades in the Dark, there are no specified attack rolls as combat is just another skill check because the focus of the game is a different one.
- In The One Ring traveling has its own mechanic with designated roles and obstacles along the way instead of just going there.
- Maybe you could add a renown system that influences all social rolls made on guild members.
