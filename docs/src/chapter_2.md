# what makes elegant ttrpg design?

So you want to make a game? Maybe it's your dozenth, or maybe you're searching for beginner advice. With this blog, I want to address both of you. I want to clearly define elegance in ttrpg design and spark a discussion on how to achieve it.

To get to the bottom of it right away, I believe elegant design depends on the intended experience. It's not necessarily making a game simple but making the *interactions* simple and delivering the desired experience with as little as possible unnecessary in-between steps. Which means that all games --- let it be ones with a focus on exploration, crafting a narrative, tactical combat, building and managing a town, or commanding a spaceship --- in their various forms can be elegant in their own right. Some might be bulkier than others but that's not what elegance refers to. It's simply a statement about implementing the mechanics, not the obligation to design your game minimalistic.

But how do you implement this well in a ttrpg? Well, I think for the sake of this question it's worthwhile to look deeper into what exactly defines ttrpgs themselves. The clearest description I ever heard was calling them "formally structured imagination"[^note] and there we have it: what sets them apart is the possibility to interact with the fictional world in whatever way comes to mind but under some constraints that turn a ttrpg into a game by forcing the players to balance their decisions. How tight these constraints are is up to the designer but I believe that regardless of the chosen approach a game can be designed elegantly to deliver the experience in the most impactful way and make every decision meaningful.
We take from this that everything should be aligned with the experience the game tries to deliver. Following that, every design decision should be made with *intent*. This does not mean you shouldn't try out things or keep accidental connections, quite the opposite, but you should always weigh up all the options.
With that, we have the foundational concepts needed to design an elegant ttrpg.

First of all, choose a theme for your game everything revolved around. This is your anchor for decision-making.
Then you identify your modes of play. Do you just have one, or different rule sets for downtime, travel, combat, or any relevant activity really. Decide what are the pillars of your game and if they need separate rules or if they fit with one another. For each mode then you distill the core experience that you want to provoke.
Think about what elements make up your experiences. Which could be achieved through narration only and which could be enhanced by dedicated mechanics?
Now we start constructing the actual game. But before implementing the mechanics though I want to point out that two aspects make up elegant design. For once the number of variables that need to be referenced for doing something and secondly the presentation of those. These are the things I will cover in the following:
Starting from the core mechanic in the main mode of play you follow a double spiral procedure. After defining your core mechanic you move on to the next most important aspect of the current mode of play. For that, you decide whether the mechanic should get its own variables or if it influences an existing one. Whenever you decide on the latter one work out a concept on how you visually present the manipulation of the mechanic so that all important information can be read at a glance. If you implement a new mechanic try to minimize non-thematic steps in playing it out (e.g. working through a list of conditions if they aren't a focus of the game) by either making it possible to work something like a damage modifier out once and writing it down somewhere to instantly work with in the future or arrange it in a way that either only one already existing value can be used or the usage only requires very simple tasks like comparing values instead of adding them up. When all elements of the first mode are covered move on to the next one if you have one and proceed the same way. Except now you also try to utilize the mechanics of the existing modes to implement new mechanics.
This way you can create a dense interconnected set of rules that enables thematic but most importantly at the same time meaningful decisions.

To give an example: in [Crown and Skull](https://www.runehammer.online/crown-skull-rpg) instead of having just a simple value for health or even a more thematic and connected wound system you cross of equipment and skills, which is both simple and easily visible when it comes into effect.
I don't want to say that every system should do it this way, but this one perfectly fits the theme and encapsulates this design philosophy.



Overview:
* find theme
* determine modes of play
* identify core experiences
* distill aspects from experiences
* decide which ones should become a mechanic
* double spiral procedure:
	* start in primary mode of play:
		* define core mechanic
		* move on to next important aspect:
			* new variables (minimize setup)
			* *or* manipulate existing one
	* move on to next most prominent mode
	* take into account completed ones





This is by no means a full guide on how to design a complete system. It's a broad overview of a possible process. I hope some found my exposition interesting and hopefully got a few new ideas. Further, ttrpg design related topics will follow on this site.
